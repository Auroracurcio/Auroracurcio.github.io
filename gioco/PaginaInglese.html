The game was invented in 1883 by the French mathematician Édouard Lucas who popularized the game below the pseudonym of N. Claus de Siam, mandarin of the college of Li-Sou-Stian. The legend according to which in a Hindu temple some monks are constantly busy moving 64 discs on three diamond columns of gold according to the rules of the Tower of Hanoi (sometimes called Tower of Brahma), was invented by the firm who first put the puzzle on the market. Legend has it that when the monks complete the work, the world will end. The minimum number of moves necessary to solve the game is in fact equivalent to 2 high to the number of discs minus 1. The monks of the legend will therefore have to complete 18,446,744,073,709,551,615 moves (264 - 1), making one move per second the world will end in 5,845,580,504 centuries. They also exist video game versions of the puzzle.

It starts with all the discs lined up on a post in descending order, to form a cone. The object of the game is to get all the discs to a different post, only being able to move one disk at a time and being able to place a disk only on another larger disk, never on a smaller one.
